-- The Kalman filter is a method that estimates a latent state from noisy observations. A common concrete application is in estimating the position of a moving vehicle. The vehicle may have an internal system that periodically measures its speed, and a separate GPS system that observes its position with some degree of error. Neither are capable of precisely locating the vehicle - but the Kalman filter provides a technique for combining these two sources of information to produce a best estimate. 

In this example, the time series of the vehicle's location is the latent state. It is not directly observable, but rather estimated from combining two sources: the internal speed data that defines the assumed evolution of the state, and the noisy GPS observations of the vehicle's location. From the vehicle's own periodic measurements of its speed, a finite difference-like estimate of its relative location can be made. However, due to the speed observations not being continuous in time, any variations in speed between observations can introduce error. In the context of Kalman filtering, this variance or uncertainty in speed fluctuations is known as the process noise - the noise associated with the evolution of the true underlying latent state (or process). Likewise, the GPS observations represent measurements, and the level of precision or uncertainty in these observations is known as observation noise. The Kalman filter seeks to balance the state evolution with the observation. In a loose sense, it averages the estimates from speed readings and GPS observations, weighting or "trusting" each based on its associated noise. 

The particle filter is a method that is similar to the Kalman filter in spirit, but offers a few advantages. One major limitation of the Kalman filter is that it assumes a linear form of the state-space equation. Additionally, it assumes the uncertain state is Gaussian, with both process noise and observation noise normally distributed. This makes it a strong tool for estimating the parameters of certain processes such as Brownian motion, but it can struggle when significant non-linearity or non-Gaussianity is introduced, such as in the Heston model of stock returns and volatility. The particle filter allows for more flexiblility by providing a nonparametric Monte Carlo-style simulation of the latent state. It simulates a series of "particles", or discrete density points, that describe possible states without necessarily following any parameteric distribution or linearity assumption. As each observation is made, the particles are each weighted according to their likelihood given the new data. Particles with high weight are resampled, providing an approximation of the posterior distribution, which gives point estimates and variances of the latent state at each point in time.


For our application, we treat the output gap as the latent state and the time series of GDP figures as the observations/measurements. We assume the output gap to evolve like an AR(1) process governed by the parameter θ and the associated white noise sequence ε, which we assume to be ~ N(0, σ^2). The GDP figures are related to the output gap at each time by the parameter φ and associated white noise sequence η ~ N(0, τ^2).
